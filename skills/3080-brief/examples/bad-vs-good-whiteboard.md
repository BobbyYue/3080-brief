# Bad vs Good Whiteboard Patterns

## Bad: Styled Text Pile

Symptoms:

- Every section is a rounded rectangle with similar weight.
- Arrows are added after the text, but do not explain causality or sequence.
- Numbers appear as a roster, not as evidence for a decision.
- Risk is a small note or missing entirely.

Fix:

- Identify the decision spine first.
- Convert relationships into visual structure: flow, matrix, hierarchy, contrast, or risk boundary.
- Make the strongest insight visually heavier.
- Keep only numbers that change the judgment.

## Bad: Pretty But Empty

Symptoms:

- Attractive shapes and colors, but little source evidence.
- The board hides caveats, scope, and risk.
- A reader remembers the style but cannot explain the decision.

Fix:

- Preserve the source's core logic, evidence, risk boundary, and next action.
- Use visual polish only to guide reading.
- Move supporting detail into the body in the simplest effective format if the opening board must stay simple.

## Good: Strategy Upgrade Board

Structure:

1. Title states the decision.
2. Left: old problem and why it fails.
3. Middle: strategy shift.
4. Right: new rule or operating model.
5. Bottom: source-backed evidence cards.
6. Side: risk boundary and next checkpoint.

Why it works:

- The visual form matches the logic of change.
- The reader can see cause, decision, evidence, and risk in one pass.
- Details are simplified, not deleted.

## Good: Data Insight Board

Structure:

1. Segment map or matrix shows where differences appear.
2. Contrast markers show which difference matters.
3. Evidence card proves the key insight.
4. Boundary box states what the data cannot prove yet.
5. Final panel turns insight into action.

Why it works:

- The board uses position and grouping, not only text.
- It preserves the insight mechanism.
- It avoids numeric inventory.
